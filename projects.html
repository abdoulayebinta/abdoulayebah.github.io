Data Warehouse

Ministry of health Liberia

Abdoulaye Binta Bah 05/01/2018

Strengthening Liberian Human Capacity for Health Services

The problem we tried to solve with this project?


The Liberian Ministry of Health was experiencing growing pains (siloed information, fragmented data). They had multiple independants health systems to gather data from, but the systems used to aggregate and analyze the data were weak and were not interoperable.

Some Data Challenges they experienced were:

  • Manually moving data from heath centers (lowest level) to district to central level (highest level)
  • Separate data silos for health worker, HIV services, human resources, and other special programs
  • Spending time and money on data collection with no ability to leverage it for strategic planning or immediate action

Since the height of the West African Ebola virus outbreak, eHealth Africa (eHA) has been working with the Liberian Ministry of Health (MOH) to rigorously improve nationwide electronic disease reporting systems, laboratory and diagnostic systems, and emergency management operations.

The Solution: a National Data Warehouse

Based on DHIS2 and a National Data Warehouse for data interoperability, the ministry has a clear, efficient system to gather view, and analyze health data at every level (community, district, county, national), and the country has what it needs to adapt and quickly react to changes or stressors in its environment.

  • Reporting completeness has improved.
  • Data managers use information to assess population health status, and health facilitites adjust their services to better meet their clients' needs.
  • As more facilitites analyze their data and act on the findings, the culture of data use continue to grow.

A data warehouse is a digital collection of integrated, context-related data used for the purpose of intelligent analysis.
Traditionally, a data warehouse is a subject-oriented, integrated, time-variant, and nonvolatile collection of data in support of management's decision making process.

    • Characteristics of the Data Warehouse

    • Subject-oriented
      • Organized around major subjects (in this case in health data)
      • Focus in analyis and modeling
      • Provide a simple and concise view around particular subject issues by excluding data that are not useful in the decision support process
    • Integrated
      • Constructed by integrating multiple, heterogenous data sources
      • Data cleaning and data integration techniques are applied
      • Data makes sense, and projects a single point of truth
    • Time-variant
      • The time horizon is significantly longer thant that of operational systems
      • The warehouse grows over time, concerned with the preservation of historical data
      • Every key structure in the data warehouse typically has an element of time
    • Non-volatile
      • Data is never removed, no exeception to this rule
      • Data is never altered; one version of truth

    Introduction to Data & Analytics

    This project is intended to provide an overview of different type of data and database technologies. The focus is on gaining the skills needed to create a complete analysis from two disparate data sources. I worked with data sets that are relevant to public health in Africa.

    Nowadays, everyone has to deal with mounds of data. People who possess a toolbox of data analysis skills have a massive edge on everyone else, because they understand what to do with all that stuff. They know how to translate raw numbers into intelligence that drives real-world action. They know how to break down and structure complex problems and data sets to get right to the heart of the problems in their business.

    Use Case

    Affordable food prices are essential to maintaining good nutrition in a population. Local market price swings are frequently reported in the news.

    The use case involves combining a food market price dataset from the United Nations Global World Food Price database with the World Health Organization’s Number of Ebola Cases and Deaths in Affected Countries dataset. Both datasets have already been downloaded from the Humanitarian Data Exchange (HDX).

    The analysis uses R to simultaneously visualize the monthly average price of locally-grown rice in five Guinea retail markets compared with the monthly average cumulative number of confirmed Ebola cases. The goal is to determine if there is a possible correlation between the 2014 Ebola outbreak and the price of rice that would justify further research. Prices swings are frequently due to changes in exchange rates and to crop harvest levels. The analysis is intented to be exploratory in nature.

    The source code for the project can be found on my Github profile.
